Key Points About Stevie Wonder's Discography:

The One-Man Band: Unlike most artists, Stevie Wonder played the vast majority of instruments on his albums—keyboards, drums, bass, synthesizers, harmonica—and was heavily involved in production and arrangement from the early 1970s onward. His classical period (1972-1976) marked the beginning of what many consider the "one-man band" era.
Early Period (1962-1968): Signed to Motown at age 11 as "Little Stevie Wonder." Released mostly producer-driven albums covering various genres (jazz, standards, novelty records). Shows incredible technical ability for his age despite varying commercial success.
Early Soul Era (1966-1971): Transitioned to hit-making, working with producer Henry Cosby. Had numerous Top 10 hits. First self-produced song ("Signed, Sealed, Delivered") in 1970 marked creative turning point. In 1971, at age 21, renegotiated Motown contract for full artistic control.
Classic Period (1972-1976): His greatest creative period. Collaborated closely with producers Robert Margouleff and Malcolm Cecil (who pioneered T.O.N.T.O. synthesizer system). Won three consecutive Grammy Awards for Album of the Year (1973, 1974, 1976). Albums: Music of My Mind, Talking Book, Innervisions, Fulfillingness' First Finale, Songs in the Key of Life. Revolutionized R&B through synthesizer use and socially conscious lyrics.
Post-Classic Transition (1979-1984): Three-year gap after Songs in the Key of Life. Experimented with instrumental/nature soundtrack. Returned with pop-oriented Hotter Than July (1980). Woman in Red soundtrack (1984) became his biggest commercial success with Oscar-winning "I Just Called to Say I Love You."
Digital/Commercial Era (1985-1995): Adopted MIDI synthesizers and drum machines. Heavy use of digital production. Albums: In Square Circle (1985), Characters (1987), and Conversation Peace (1995). More commercial pop-soul focus. Less frequent releases—significant gaps between albums became common.
Notable Absence: A Time to Love (2005) was his most recent studio album—19 years ago. Since then, Stevie Wonder has remained active with live performances, guest appearances, and political advocacy but has not released another studio album, making this an extended creative hiatus or possible retirement from studio recording.
Guest Collaborators: Throughout his career, Stevie selectively featured guest musicians. His classic period included guitarists Jeff Beck and Ray Parker Jr. His 1980s albums featured R&B/soul vocalists like Luther Vandross, Philip Bailey, and Syreeta Wright. Despite this, Stevie remained the primary creative and instrumental force on nearly all recordings.
